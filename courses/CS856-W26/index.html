Overview

Transport protocols are an essential, complex, and continuously evolving part of network communications. They bridge the gap between distributed applications and the lossy, best-effort network layer by providing key services such as data segmentation and reassembly, data loss detection and recovery, flow control, and congestion control. These protocols handle every piece of data exchanged between application processes on different endpoints. They are asynchronous and event-driven, have to handle numerous nontrivial corner cases to account for the changing network and receiver conditions and packet losses and delays, and significantly impact the network quality of service experienced by distributed applications and online services using the network. As such, it is critical to develop an accurate understanding of the performance characteristics of transport protocols under different application workloads and network conditions prior to deployment.

In this course, through course projects and presentations, we will do a deep dive on recent advances in network transport protocols and analyze and explore their performance characteristics. This performance exploration, which will be part of the course project and student presentations, can be analytical or empirical, depending on the individual student's interest and skills. Students can pick their focus from a wide range of protocols, including but not limited to:

  • The "classic" TCP and its many variants
  • QUIC, developed for faster web transfers
  • RDMA over Converged Ethernet (RoCE)
  • Receiver-driven transport protocols proposed for RPC-like traffic in data centers (e.g., Homa, dcPIM, NDP)
  • Recent proposals for customized network transport for AI workloads
  • < your favorite protocol >

Grading

Final grades will be based on the following components:

  • Presentations: 35%
  • Participation in discussions: 15%
  • Final project report: 50%

For the presentation component, students are expected to deeply understand their chosen protocol and associated papers and lead a full class discussion (60-75 minutes). One week before the presentation, students must send the instructor a presentation plan, see slides for details.

Schedule

This course is structured around a semester-long project on transport protocols. The schedule below outlines the major milestones. See the slides for the inroductory lectures for details. The exact order of topics and presentation dates may be adjusted during the term.

Weeks 1–2: Introductory lectures [week1][week2]. In the first week, each student will select a transport protocol and a network setting to study in depth over the term.

First half of the term (roughly until Reading Week): Students will learn about and teach others their chosen protocol and network setting. This phase focuses on:

  • Presenting the protocol and its design goals
  • Modeling the protocol in a domain-specific language (DSL)
  • Clearly specifying assumptions and properties of the target applications and network
  • Getting started on reproducing results from the original paper to establish an experimental setup

Second half of the term: We will explore the effectiveness of the chosen protocols by critically evaluating their assumptions and experimental setups. Students will examine:

  • Whether the evaluated workloads and network settings are appropriate
  • What baselines the protocol is compared against
  • What scenarios or workloads may be missing
  • Conditions under which the protocol may perform poorly or undesirably

Final Report: A 6-page final project report is due at the end of the term (April 23).

Alternative project option: One group of up to two students can instead build a unified network simulation environment for evaluating transport protocols (e.g., using ns-3). This group will:

  • Use one class session (instead of the first presentation) to demo the simulator
  • Support other students in using the simulator throughout the term
  • Continue improving the simulator during the second half of the course
  • Submit the same 6-page final project report
